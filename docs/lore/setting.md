## 1. One-line pitch

A long-dead galactic civilization tried to power its ascension by draining a parallel universe — and failed catastrophically. Millennia later the wound is still bleeding: cosmic storms of foreign physics, alien monsters from over there, fragments of the dead echoing in the ruins. The only stable points are the ancient Lighthouses they left behind, and fleet captains compete to claim them — quietly, before whatever lives on the other side notices again.

## 2. Tone & creative pillars

### What this universe is

- **Salvage-pulp.** Outer-rim scrappers in a 70s pulp sci-fi world. Rusted junker hulls, chrome psychic cruisers, and bio-organic alien ships all canonical. The aesthetic is grimy and lived-in — post-collapse but not dystopian.
- **Weird without being grim.** The cosmology is huge and tragic, but day-to-day life is adventure. People still tell jokes. The fog and monsters are facts of life, not constant oppression.
- **Mystery is a feature.** Nobody knows everything about the K3 civilization, the other side, or how Lighthouses really work. Characters hypothesize and disagree. Authors stay aligned via this doc; characters in-fiction can stay confused.
- **Small but capable.** The player is one of thousands of fleet captains. Not chosen, not destined. But they're skilled, and that's enough. The contrast between cosmic scale and personal scale is the engine.

### What this universe is not

- Not Star Wars: no Jedi-tier metaphysics, no clean Empire-versus-Rebels binary.
- Not 40k: rusted, not nihilistic.
- Not hard SF: physics is hand-waved. The battery-universe is fiction, not technology.
- Not cosmic horror: the tone leans pulp adventure with horror beats, never the reverse.
- Not battle-magic anime: psychics are subtle (perception, communion, navigation), not damage-dealers.

### Voice for writers

- **Show grime, not decay.** Specific details over abstract dread. "Plating scavenged from a freighter that died in the '08 storms" beats "the ship was old and battered."
- **Weather is alive.** Storms get names; fogs get moods. Treat foreign physics as a character.
- **Names are pulp.** Punchy crew callsigns ("Sixgun"), ship names ("Carbon Mongoose"), system names ("Veyra Hesper"). Slightly grease-stained.
- **Don't over-explain.** Characters in-fiction know fragments. Players learn the universe through scattered evidence, not exposition.
- **Echoes speak archaically.** K3 echoes are formal, oblique, slightly out of phase with modern speech.

## 3. The Cascade

The galaxy was once unified under a single Kardashev-3 civilization — humans or proto-humans, immensely advanced, capable of harnessing the energy output of every star they had touched. They had no rivals, no remaining frontiers, and one final ambition: to ascend to Kardashev-4 — the power of an entire universe, not just a galaxy. Their solution was to tap a parallel universe as a battery and let it pour power into ours.

The plan worked, briefly. Then it cascaded.

First the other side noticed. The drain was not flowing into uninhabited space. Something on the far end — a mind, or many minds, or just the rules of that place — recognized the theft and counter-attacked through the connection. The seal that was supposed to keep the parallel universe locked away broke under the strain. The flow reversed in places, oscillated wildly in others, and finally tore wide. Foreign physics began bleeding into ours. The K3 civilization, whose entire infrastructure was tied into the connection, was pulled with the energy as it cascaded — minds dragged across the breach in fragments, bodies left behind in vast empty cities, machines still humming in stellar orbits with no one at the helm. Within what later historians would call a single year, the galaxy went silent.

Millennia have passed. The wound did not close. Cosmic storms of foreign physics still drift through systems, monsters bred in the bleed zones still hunt the shipping lanes, and the over-there minds — distant, ancient, occasionally aware — still notice when the rules thin enough to let them through. The Lighthouses the K3 left behind, originally built to manage the connection, still hum where they're activated, holding local reality in place against the slow drain. They are the only reason most systems are habitable at all.

## 4. The galaxy today

### Three bands, one network

Travel between systems requires a Lighthouse. Without one to lock onto, a drive cannot punch through the foreign-physics fog that fills interstellar space — the ship simply vanishes into the bleed and nothing comes back. Activated Lighthouses form a network. The density of that network defines the political geography of the modern galaxy.

**The inner systems** are densely Lighthouse-linked. Most Lighthouses here were activated within a few centuries of the Cascade, when the first survivor populations rediscovered them and pieced together the activation rituals. Society had time to stabilize. Today the inner systems host the post-Cascade nations — a handful of re-coalesced civilizations, each spanning multiple star systems, each old enough to have its own languages, customs, and political institutions.

**The middle band** has Lighthouses but the network is patchier. Activation came later, often only because a corporate consortium or expedition force funded the work. Here the dominant powers are corps — the Salvage Combine, the Beacon Reclamation Trust, and others — that hold trade routes between the inner nations and the rim. Corporate space tends to be lawful enough that contracts pay out and unlawful enough that nobody asks where your fleet's reactor came from.

**The outer rim** is mosaic. Lighthouses out here are isolated — sometimes a single working beacon stands as the only link between an entire frontier hold and the rest of the galaxy. Power is regional and unstable: salvage gangs, micro-states, refugee colonies, weather-cults that hold a system as scripture. New Lighthouse activations still happen out here — most of the unclaimed beacons left in the galaxy are rim systems no one's gotten around to. This is where most fleet captains start.

### The Crossroads-Beacon

At the geographic center of the inner systems sits an unusually intact K3 artifact: a Lighthouse far larger than the others, partially still warm from whatever it was originally built to do. The city that grew up around it over millennia is called the **Crossroads-Beacon** — or just the Beacon. It's the largest single inhabited structure in the galaxy, a layered metropolis of habitats and embassies stacked around a column of pre-Cascade machinery that nobody fully understands.

The Beacon is run by the **Lighthouse Authority** — a treaty-bound council funded jointly by the inner nations, the major corps, and (uneasily) several of the larger weather-cults. The Authority is not a nation and has no military of its own. It maintains the Beacon as neutral ground, certifies new Wardens who claim Lighthouses, and routes universe-wide announcements through the Beacon's broadcast network. Every nation, corp, and major frontier hold maintains an office or embassy somewhere in the metropolis. Most fleet captains visit at least once.

## 5. Inhabitants

Three flavors of person walk the post-Cascade galaxy. The lines between them are clearer in some places (the inner nations track citizenship by biology) than others (the rim doesn't ask).

**Humans** are the baseline and the majority. Modern humans are the distant inheritors of the K3 civilization — the population that survived the Cascade and clawed back across millennia. Long isolation in single systems before Lighthouse re-activation produced regional variation: zero-G adapted frames in some nations, weather-touched bloodlines (the rare psychic Inheritors) elsewhere, sub-cultures that came back from near-extinction with new languages and customs. They look human enough to recognize and human enough to disagree with.

**Aliens** evolved separately — some likely existed before the Cascade and survived it (their worlds were just lucky enough to ride out the chaos), others speciated rapidly in the millennia since under foreign-physics weather pressure. Several distinct species are scattered across the galaxy, each with its own biology: bio-organic crew who engineer their own ships from living tissue, exotic-physiology species adapted to atmospheres or gravities humans can't tolerate, others stranger still. No alien civilization rivals the inner-systems nations in scale, but several hold their own systems and contribute crew, ships, and visual variety to the galactic mix.

**K3 echoes** are the most contested category. When the Cascade dragged the K3 civilization across the breach, fragments stayed behind — minds without bodies, bodies without minds, chunks of consciousness embedded in pre-Cascade machinery still waiting in the ruins. Sometimes that machinery wakes up. An echo can be a stilted person who walks out of a sealed bunker after ten thousand years of dormancy; it can be a shipboard intelligence that introduces itself in archaic dialect; it can be a cluster of distributed memory that briefly speaks through a Lighthouse's broadcast equipment. Echoes are formal, oblique, and partial — none of them remember everything they were. The chrome psychic cruisers favored by the larger weather-cults are echo-built, sold or gifted by an echo that decided to enter the modern economy. Some inner-systems nations grant echoes citizenship; others deport or hunt them as cursed. Most of them, when asked, prefer to keep walking.

## 6. Psychics & the cults

Psychic ability in the post-Cascade galaxy comes in three tiers, distinguished by how the gift was acquired. None of them are common.

**Inheritors** are the rarest and strongest. Their ancestors stood near the Cascade when it broke — close enough to be marked, distant enough to survive — and the mark passed down. Inheritor abilities tend to be narrow and consistent within a bloodline: one family reads the weather, another talks to echoes, another has a knack for finding intact ruins. Most Inheritor lines have died out over the millennia, diluted or culled. The few left are highly visible figures, often working as advisors to nations or major corps.

**Cult-trained** psychics are the learnable variant. With sustained exposure to foreign physics — meditation in fog zones, prolonged proximity to active K3 artifacts, ritual practice in the deep weather — most people can develop modest abilities. Several major weather-cults run training pipelines and gatekeep their methods. They are also factions: the cults compete for territory, recruits, and influence over which Lighthouses get prioritized for Authority funding. A cult-trained psychic is rarely as strong as an Inheritor, but there are far more of them, and their training is reproducible.

**Touched** psychics are the wild card. Born during a storm. Lost in fog as a child and walked out three days later changed. Brushed by a monster and survived. The ability arrives without warning and rarely with a manual. Touched psychics are sometimes recruited by cults, sometimes feared, sometimes destroyed by their own gift before they figure out what it is.

A note on the cults' theology, which the cults themselves don't realize: each major cult is, in fact, communing with a *different* over-there mind — a Sleeper, a Drowned Choir, a Conductor — without knowing the others exist. Each cult believes it has the true picture. Each is partly right. None are willing to compare notes.

What psychics can do, in practice, is bounded. They sense storms before instruments do. They navigate fog zones intuitively. They can sometimes coax an echo into speaking, or pull a clean signal out of weather noise. They are perception, communion, and influence — not battle-magi. A fleet captain values a psychic on the bridge the way they value a good navigator: they read what the instruments can't.

## 7. The Other Side

What lies through the Cascade breach has never been mapped. Psychics report fragmentary impressions — vast distances, slow cyclonic structures, frequencies that resolve into voices when listened to long enough — and the impressions contradict each other. The Lighthouse Authority's official line is that no one knows. Privately, every weather-cult disagrees.

What is known: the other side has both *minds* and *terrain*, and both can reach across.

### The minds

A handful of vast intelligences drift in the rifts on the far side. They are ancient — old enough that their interest in our universe is unhurried — and they are partial: each appears to perceive only the slice of our reality nearest its own location. Three are named in the cult literature, though the names are guesses, and the cults that worship one would deny they're worshipping a stranger.

- **The Sleeper.** Slow, vast, mostly indifferent. Stirs once or twice a generation. When it does, an entire stretch of inner-systems Lighthouses falters. Its dreams sometimes leak through as compulsions in nearby psychics.
- **The Drowned Choir.** Plural, layered, polyphonic — a cluster of minds running in parallel that finish each other's thoughts. Communicates in singing. Its cult is the largest of the weather-cults; the cult does not know the rest of the Choir exists.
- **The Conductor.** Active, attentive, dangerous. Probes the breach deliberately, looking for thin spots. The recurring high-tier villains in the modern era are usually the Conductor's incursions — directed monsters, possessed echoes, occasional architectural changes to entire star systems that no one ordered.

There are likely others — fewer, farther, stranger — that no cult has yet found.

### The terrain

Most of what bleeds through is not directed. The other side has its own physics — different rules for matter, time, and causality — and where it touches ours, those rules briefly apply. Foreign-physics weather drifts through our systems as cosmic storms. Native organisms that evolved in the rifts wander into our space and become the giant monsters of shipping-lane legend. Patches of ambient leakage become haunted regions where instruments lie. None of these are aware of us in any sense humans would recognize. They are autonomous, often dangerous, and *normal* — the post-Cascade galaxy treats them the way old earthly sailors treated weather and reefs. Ordinary hazards that occasionally kill you.

## 8. The Lighthouse network

The Lighthouses are the only piece of K3 infrastructure that still works.

Every star system has at least one — vast tower-like structures usually parked in stable orbit around the system's primary star, occasionally on a rocky moon or a Lagrange point. They were built before the Cascade as part of the K3 civilization's harness over the parallel-universe drain. What they originally did is contested; what they do now is well-attested. An activated Lighthouse:

- **Anchors local reality.** Within a radius around the Lighthouse — usually the whole star system — foreign-physics leakage thins and behaves predictably. Ships fly. Weather recedes. Monsters thin out. Without an active Lighthouse, a star system is functionally uninhabitable.
- **Joins the network.** Activated Lighthouses signal each other across the breach-blocking fog. Two ships can lock onto separate Lighthouses and traverse the storm-laden interstellar dark between them. This is the *only* form of practical interstellar travel in the modern galaxy. A system without an active Lighthouse is unreachable except by sublight, which for most distances takes longer than a civilization can sustain.
- **Accepts upgrades.** Pre-Cascade tech recovered from K3 ruins can be installed in a Lighthouse's reception spires. Each upgrade extends what the Lighthouse can do — wider fog clearance, weather damping, longer-range network links, defensive measures against incursions, occasional stranger effects. The upgrade catalog is partial, scattered across thousands of ruins, and not fully understood. New finds still surface.

### Wardens

A Lighthouse without a Warden runs at minimum power. It anchors the system and stays on the network, but it's vulnerable: it doesn't refuse claims, it doesn't defend itself, and most upgrade slots stay empty. Most rim-system Lighthouses sit in this state. Anyone who completes the right Authority-vetted contracts can become the Warden of an unclaimed Lighthouse. Wardens earn tribute from activity in their system, prioritize which upgrades the Lighthouse accepts, and become the system's nominal authority. They are also responsible for defending it.

### Threats

- **Villain incursions.** A Sleeper-stir or Conductor-directed attack can target a specific Lighthouse, attempting to seize or unmake it. If the attack succeeds, the system suffers the consequences: the network connection may sever, debuffs settle in, and the Warden, if any, loses the seat. Specifics in Phase 3e.
- **Inactivity.** A Warden who abandons their system for too long loses the seat — the Lighthouse de-recognizes them and reverts to unclaimed.
- **Other Wardens.** Direct Warden-vs-Warden challenges are not part of the modern era's etiquette. Yet. (Phase 4.)

Specifics — upgrade catalogs, claim mechanics, defense outcomes — live in the Phase 3b and 3e specs. The setting commits to: Lighthouses are pre-Cascade artifacts, every system has one, controlling the Lighthouse means controlling the system, and the network is the only thing keeping the galaxy navigable.

## 9. The player's place

The player is a freelance fleet captain — one of thousands operating across the galaxy in the modern post-Cascade era. They have a starter ship, a small crew, and no patrons, lineage, or destiny that distinguishes them from the rest. What they have is competence and ambition.

Most captains spend their careers as scrappers, contractors, and mercenaries — running jobs for the corps in middle-band space, hauling for inner-systems nations, hunting weather-monsters for cult or Authority bounties on the rim. Some never aim higher than a steady paycheque. The ones who do — who have the patience to take Authority-vetted contracts in unclaimed rim systems, the crew to win them, and the ships to defend what they take — eventually claim a Lighthouse and become Wardens.

The galaxy has more empty Lighthouses than serious candidates for them. The path is open. Becoming a Warden is not legendary; defending one for years against weather, villains, and rival captains is.

## 10. Deferred to specs

The setting commits to the cosmology, tone, and named entities required for cross-phase coherence. Many concrete details remain open and should be settled in the spec for the phase that needs them.

**Phase 3b (Lighthouses + System Character):**

- Lighthouse upgrade catalog — what categories exist, how many tiers, how acquired
- Star type effects (single / binary / trinary, color, age) on system character
- Planet / system-feature taxonomy and yield mechanics
- Activation mechanics — what "claiming" a Lighthouse looks like in commands

**Phase 3c (Resource Loop):**

- Resource categories, yield rates, decay
- Planet-exploitation gameplay verbs

**Phase 3d (Home Base):**

- Home base structure and progression
- Which existing commands consolidate into the base UI

**Phase 3e (Events: Weather + Channel + Villains):**

- Specific weather event taxonomy and durations
- Named villains beyond the placeholder over-there minds
- Specific named nations, corps, and weather-cults
- Job board content tiers and rotation cadence

**Phase 4 (Combat depth + PvP):**

- Combat resolution depth (turn-by-turn beyond stat math)
- Warden-vs-Warden challenge mechanics

**Soft commitments worth flagging.** The setting commits to humans-as-K3-inheritors at the cosmological level (§3) and to three named over-there minds (§7) as recurring antagonists. These are renameable / replaceable when phase specs need them — the setting's job is to make sure the *categories* exist and are consistent. Specific names can be swapped without re-litigating the categories.
